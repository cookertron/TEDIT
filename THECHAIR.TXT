The Chair
A Story of Time, Survival, and the Long Way Home
________________


Part One: The Find
The chair was ugly. There was no other word for it.


Elliot Wynn found it on a Tuesday afternoon in late September, half-buried in a thicket of blackberry bushes behind the abandoned Hargrove property at the end of Moss Lane. He was twelve years old, small for his age, with a mess of dark hair that his mother had given up trying to tame. He'd been looking for his dog, Biscuit — a barrel-chested beagle with a talent for disappearing — when he spotted the thing through a gap in the thorns.


It was an armchair. Or it had been, once. The fabric was a faded burgundy, worn through to the stuffing in patches like continents on a strange map. One of its wooden legs was splintered and wrapped in what looked like copper wire. The armrests were scuffed down to bare wood on the tops, as though a thousand hands had gripped them. And carved into the back, almost invisible beneath decades of grime, was a symbol Elliot didn't recognise — a circle with a line through it, bisected again by a shorter line, like a compass that had forgotten which way was north.


"Biscuit?" Elliot called again. His voice sounded thin in the overgrown garden. The Hargrove house loomed behind him, its windows like dead eyes.


The dog didn't come. The chair sat there.


Elliot was the kind of boy who picked things up. Broken radios, discarded watches, bicycle chains — his bedroom was a museum of salvaged junk that his father called "a health hazard" and his mother called "creative." He couldn't leave the chair. It was too strange, too solidly there among the weeds, as though it had been waiting.


He dragged it free of the brambles, scratching his arms bloody in the process, and hauled it across the garden, through the gap in the fence, and down the lane to his house. It was heavier than it looked. By the time he got it into the garage, his shoulders ached and his palms were raw and he was sweating through his school shirt.


He sat down in it to catch his breath.


Nothing happened. Of course nothing happened. It was a chair.


Elliot leaned back. The cushion sagged beneath him with a familiar creak, and he felt the strange rightness of it — the way the armrests met his elbows at exactly the right height, the way the back curved to fit the shape of his spine. As though it had been built for him. He closed his eyes, just for a second.


He opened them to a world on fire.

Part Two: The Cretaceous
The sky was wrong.


That was Elliot's first thought. Not where am I or what happened — those came later, crashing in like waves. His first thought, stupid and clear, was that the sky was the wrong colour. It was a thick, heavy orange, streaked with ribbons of cloud so dark they were almost purple, and the sun — if it was the sun — sat enormous and swollen near the horizon, bleeding light across a landscape that looked nothing like Moss Lane.


He was in a clearing. The ground beneath the chair's legs was dark, almost black soil, damp and spongy. Around him, trees rose to dizzying heights — not oaks or elms or anything he could name, but towering things with rough, scaly bark and crowns of feathery green fronds that spread out like umbrellas against that bruised sky. The air was hot. Not English-summer hot. Hot like an oven, thick with moisture, and it smelled of rot and green things and something else — something sharp and animal that made his stomach clench.


Elliot gripped the armrests.


"Okay," he whispered. "Okay, okay, okay."


He was still in the chair. The chair was still beneath him, solid and real, its burgundy fabric absurdly mundane against the prehistoric jungle. Because that's what this was. He knew it with a certainty that bypassed logic and went straight to his bones. This wasn't a dream. This wasn't a prank. He had read enough books, watched enough documentaries, built enough models of dinosaurs from kits his grandmother sent him at Christmas to know what he was looking at.


This was old. This was ancient.


A sound came from the trees — a low, resonant boom that Elliot felt in his ribcage more than heard. Then another, closer. The fronds of the nearest tree shivered, and something moved in the shadows between the trunks. Something enormous.


Elliot did the only thing he could think of. He closed his eyes, gripped the armrests until his knuckles turned white, and wished — desperately, furiously — to go home.


When he opened his eyes, the jungle was gone.


________________




He was somewhere else.


The chair sat on a flat expanse of grey stone under a sky so pale it was almost white. There was no sun visible, just a diffuse, directionless light that made everything look flat and shadowless. The air was cold — bitterly, shockingly cold after the jungle's heat — and dry as paper. In every direction, the stone stretched to the horizon, broken only by low ridges and shallow depressions filled with something that might have been frost or might have been salt. There was no sound at all. No wind. No insects. No distant boom of ancient creatures. Nothing.


Elliot stood up from the chair. His legs were shaking. He turned in a slow circle, and the emptiness turned with him, and he understood with a sickening lurch that he was alone in a way he had never been alone before.


"Hello?" he called.


His voice fell flat against the stone and died.


This was before life, he thought. Or after it. One of the vast, barren stretches of Earth's history where nothing grew and nothing breathed and the planet was just a rock spinning in the dark. He was standing on a world that wouldn't know a human footprint for millions — maybe billions — of years.


The cold was already seeping through his school shirt. He could feel it in his fingers, in his toes, crawling up his arms. He had no coat. No food. No water. He had nothing but a ruined chair and the growing, nauseating conviction that he had no idea how to get home.


He sat back down. He closed his eyes.


The chair took him again.

Part Three: Learning the Rules
Over the next several hours — or what felt like hours; time had become a slippery, unreliable thing — Elliot learned three facts about the chair.


One: Every time he sat in it after standing up, it moved him. Not through space, as far as he could tell, but through time. He always seemed to land in the same spot on the Earth's surface, but the Earth itself was unrecognisably different each time. The landscape changed. The sky changed. The air changed.


Two: He had no control over where — or rather, when — it took him. He tried thinking about home. He tried thinking about specific dates. He tried thinking about nothing at all. It made no difference. The chair went where it wanted.


Three: The odds were against him.


This was the fact that settled in his stomach like a stone. Earth was four and a half billion years old. Human civilization — the part with houses and roads and his mum's kitchen and Biscuit sleeping on the sofa — occupied a sliver so thin it was almost nothing. A few thousand years out of billions. If the chair was picking moments at random, the chances of landing in his own time were so small they might as well have been zero.


He understood this on his seventh jump, sitting in the chair on a beach of black volcanic sand while the sea — a strange, greenish sea under a methane sky — lapped at the chair's legs. He was hungry. He was thirsty. He was so frightened that his hands wouldn't stop trembling.


"Please," he said to the chair. To the sky. To whatever was listening. "Please take me home."


He sat. He jumped.


He landed in a swamp.


________________


Part Four: The Carboniferous
The air hit him like a wall — dense, wet, and so rich with oxygen it made him dizzy. Elliot gasped and grabbed the armrests as the world swam around him, every colour too bright, every edge too sharp, as though someone had turned up the contrast on reality.


He was in a forest. But not a forest like any he had ever seen. The trees were enormous — not just tall but thick, their trunks wider than cars, covered in a bark that was more like the texture of a pineapple than anything that belonged on a tree. They soared upward and disappeared into a canopy so dense that the light filtering through was green and watery, like being at the bottom of a pond. Between them, giant ferns unfurled fronds the size of bedsheets, and the ground was a carpet of moss so deep that the chair had sunk into it up to its armrests.


It was warm. Not the crushing heat of the Cretaceous, but a thick, tropical warmth that clung to his skin. And it was loud — a wall of sound made up of clicks, buzzes, and a strange rhythmic chirping that came from everywhere and nowhere.


Then he saw the dragonfly.


It drifted out from behind a fern and hovered in front of him, and Elliot stopped breathing. Its wingspan was as wide as his arm. Its body was the length of his forearm, segmented and iridescent, shimmering blue-green in the filtered light. Its eyes were enormous, multifaceted, catching the light like dark jewels. It hung in the air on wings that moved too fast to see, watching him with what felt horribly like intelligence.


Elliot didn't move. The dragonfly regarded him for a long moment, then banked lazily to the left and disappeared into the canopy. He let out a shaking breath.


Carboniferous, he thought. Three hundred million years ago, give or take. The age of giant insects, when the oxygen levels were so high that arthropods grew to monstrous sizes. He remembered a poster on his bedroom wall — Life Through the Ages — and the illustration of a Meganeura, a dragonfly with a seventy-centimetre wingspan.


He'd just seen one. He'd just seen one.


A laugh bubbled up in his chest — high, thin, slightly unhinged. He was lost. He was alone. He was almost certainly going to die. But he had just seen a Meganeura, and for one brief, shining moment, he felt something that wasn't fear.


It was wonder.


He decided not to jump again immediately. He was exhausted, and the air here — though alien — was breathable, even pleasant. The oxygen richness made him feel alert, almost euphoric. There was water pooling in the depressions between the tree roots, dark but clear, and after a long hesitation, he cupped his hands and drank. It tasted of earth and moss and nothing harmful.


Food was harder. He didn't recognise anything that looked edible. No fruits, no berries — just endless ferns and mosses and the towering, alien trees. His stomach cramped, and he pressed his hand against it and tried not to think about the leftover pasta in the fridge at home, about his mother calling him down for dinner, about the sound of the television in the living room and the smell of toast.


He slept in the chair that night, curled awkwardly in its embrace, while the Carboniferous forest clicked and hummed around him. The darkness was absolute. There was no moon — or if there was, the canopy hid it. Things moved in the undergrowth, rustling through the moss, and once something large splashed in the water nearby, and Elliot pulled his knees to his chest and didn't sleep again until dawn.


When the light came back, grey and green through the canopy, he made himself stand. He explored a little, never going far enough to lose sight of the chair. He found mushrooms growing on a fallen log — pale, fleshy things the size of dinner plates — and he ate one, because the hunger had become a bright, insistent pain behind his ribs, and because he decided that dying of poison was at least faster than dying of starvation.


The mushroom didn't kill him. It tasted like wet cardboard, but it sat in his stomach without rebellion, and after an hour he ate another.


He spent two days in the Carboniferous. Two days of mushrooms and mossy water and the constant, overwhelming presence of insects. He saw millipedes as long as his leg, armoured and slow, trundling through the undergrowth. He saw scorpions the size of cats. He saw things he couldn't name — multi-legged, chitinous things that scuttled in the shadows and made sounds like paper tearing.


On the second night, he woke to find something on his leg. It was a centipede, easily a metre long, its body a chain of dark, glossy segments, each pair of legs moving in a ripple that he could feel through the fabric of his trousers. He held his breath. It explored his knee, his thigh, the edge of the chair, its antennae flickering. Then it moved on, flowing off him like water, and disappeared into the dark.


Elliot cried. Quietly, with his hand pressed over his mouth, he cried until his chest ached and his eyes were swollen and there was nothing left. Then he wiped his face on his sleeve, sat properly in the chair, and jumped.

Part Five: Fire and Ice
The next jump took him to a world of fire.


The ground was black and cracked, and through the cracks, orange light pulsed like a heartbeat. The air shimmered with heat, and in the distance, a range of low mountains glowed red at their summits, pouring rivers of molten rock down their flanks in lazy, luminous streams. The sky was choked with ash — a solid ceiling of grey that turned the light a dull, apocalyptic amber.


Elliot didn't even stand up. The heat was unbearable, pressing against his skin like a physical weight, and the air tasted of sulphur and metal. He could feel the soles of his shoes getting hot. The chair's legs, planted on the volcanic rock, were starting to char.


He sat. He jumped.


Snow.


A world of white, so bright it blinded him. He threw his arm over his eyes and felt the cold slam into him, instant and merciless, a cold so profound it seemed to reach inside his body and squeeze his lungs. Wind screamed across a flat, featureless plain of ice, driving needles of snow into any exposed skin. The chair rocked, and for a terrifying moment he thought it would tip over, and he would be thrown onto the ice, and the chair would skid away from him across the frozen plain, and he would be stranded in an ice age with nothing.


He grabbed the armrests. He sat. He jumped.


Jungle. Ocean. Desert. Swamp.


He jumped and jumped, and each time the world was different and each time it was wrong — too old, too young, too hot, too cold, too empty. He lost count of the jumps. He lost count of the days. Hunger became a permanent companion, a hollow ache that lived behind his ribs and coloured everything grey. Thirst was worse. There were jumps where he landed in places with no water at all — barren moonscapes of rock and dust where the air was so dry it cracked his lips and made his nose bleed.


He learned to act fast. The moment he landed, he would scan the landscape for water, for anything that looked edible, for danger. He became a scavenger, a forager, an animal. He ate things that would have made him gag a week ago — grubs pulled from rotting wood, raw fish scooped from shallow streams with his bare hands, the tough, bitter roots of plants he couldn't name. He drank from puddles, from rivers, from the cupped leaves of giant ferns. He got sick. Twice, he vomited so hard he thought his stomach would turn inside out. Once, something he ate gave him a fever that lasted through three jumps, and he lay in the chair shivering and sweating and delirious, seeing things in the sky that weren't there — his mother's face, his bedroom ceiling, the familiar, stupid shape of Biscuit sleeping on his bed.


But he didn't die. Somehow, impossibly, he didn't die.

Part Six: The Devonian Shore
He woke to the sound of water.


Not the distant roar of a river or the patter of rain, but the gentle, rhythmic hush of waves on a shore. Elliot opened his eyes. He was on a beach — a real beach, with sand that was almost white and water that stretched to the horizon under a sky of pale, clear blue. The chair sat at the tide line, its legs dark with wet sand.


He stood up carefully, his body stiff and aching from days of being curled in the chair. The air was warm but not oppressive, and it smelled of salt and something organic — a rich, living smell, like a rockpool at low tide.


The sea was calm. Small waves lapped at the sand, leaving lines of foam that fizzed and dissolved. And in the shallows, Elliot could see life — not the vast, terrifying life of the dinosaur age or the monstrous insects of the Carboniferous, but small, tentative things. Creatures with shells spiralled and ridged. Dark shapes that might have been fish, flickering in the deeper water. And on the wet sand at the water's edge, something that made him drop to his knees.


Tracks.


Not footprints, exactly. More like the marks left by something dragging itself — a pattern of shallow grooves and the impressions of stubby, splayed limbs. Something had crawled out of the water and onto the land.


Elliot followed the tracks up the beach. They led to a tumble of rocks at the base of a low cliff, and there, in a shallow depression, he found it — a creature about the size of his forearm, flat and broad, with a blunt head and four stumpy legs that ended in something halfway between fins and feet. Its skin was dark and wet, glistening in the sunlight. It looked up at him with small, round eyes and didn't move.


A tetrapod. One of the first. One of the animals that had crawled out of the sea and onto the land and begun the long, improbable chain of events that would lead, eventually, unfathomably, to him.


Elliot sat down on the rocks and looked at it, and it looked at him, and something passed between them that he couldn't name. He was looking at his own ancestor — not literally, maybe, but close enough. This small, glistening, ridiculous creature was the beginning of everything. Every mammal, every reptile, every bird, every human who had ever lived or ever would live had started here, in this moment, on this beach, with this brave and stupid decision to leave the water.


"Hello," Elliot said softly.


The creature blinked at him. Then it turned, with a laborious, waddling motion, and dragged itself back toward the sea.


Elliot watched it go. The waves took it, and it was gone.


He stayed on that beach for three days. The water was full of fish — small, bony, primitive things — and he caught them with his hands in the shallows, the way he'd learned to do in the Carboniferous streams. He ate them raw, crouched on the sand, tearing the flesh from the bones with his teeth. It wasn't good, but it was food, and after so many days of hunger, each mouthful was a victory.


He slept on the sand next to the chair, with the sound of the waves in his ears, and for the first time in what felt like forever, he didn't dream of home. He dreamed of nothing at all, and woke rested, and sat watching the sunrise — deep gold over a blue-green sea — and felt something open in his chest.


Not happiness, exactly. Not peace. But something close. An acceptance, maybe. A settling.


He was here. He was alive. That was enough, for now.

Part Seven: The Ordovician and the Dark Water
The peace didn't last. It never did.


The next jump dropped him into a world of water. The chair appeared on a narrow spit of rock barely wider than Elliot's outstretched arms, surrounded on all sides by a dark, still ocean. The sky was overcast and low, and the air smelled of brine and something faintly metallic.


There was no land visible. No horizon except the flat line where grey water met grey sky. The rock was slick with something that might have been algae — a thin, greenish film that made every step treacherous.


Elliot's heart hammered. He had no food, no fresh water, and the ocean stretched out in every direction like a sentence with no end. He looked down into the water. It was clear near the surface, and he could see things moving in it — pale shapes that drifted and pulsed, trailing filaments behind them like ghostly hair. Jellyfish, maybe. Or something older. Things without names that had lived and died hundreds of millions of years before anyone was around to name them.


Something larger moved in the deeper water. A shadow, long and tapered, gliding beneath the surface with a slow, purposeful motion. Elliot pulled his feet back from the edge.


He sat in the chair. He didn't want to stay.


But nothing happened.


He stood up. Sat down again. Nothing. The chair remained stubbornly, terrifyingly on the rock, and the ocean lapped at its edges, and the shadow in the water circled.


For the first time, the chair refused to move.


Panic hit him like a fist. He stood up, sat down, stood up, sat down — again and again, each time more frantic, each time more desperate, his breath coming in short, ragged gasps. The chair didn't move. The chair was just a chair, sitting on a rock in the middle of an ancient ocean, and he was going to die here, alone, surrounded by water he couldn't drink and creatures that had no concept of mercy.


He screamed. He screamed until his throat was raw, a wordless, animal sound that the grey sky swallowed whole. Then he slumped forward, his forehead against the chair's back, and sobbed.


The shadow in the water came closer. He could see it now — a shape like a flattened cone, segmented, with rows of undulating fins along its sides and a cluster of appendages at the front that could have been legs or mandibles or something else entirely. It was at least two metres long. It circled the rock, closer with each pass, its body rippling through the water with a mechanical precision that was somehow worse than speed.


Elliot watched it. He was too tired to be afraid. That was the thing about fear — it burned hot, but it couldn't burn forever. Eventually it left behind only ash, and the ash was a kind of numbness that felt almost like calm.


"Okay," he said. His voice was hoarse. "Okay. Come on, then."


The creature circled. The water lapped. The sky pressed down.


He sat in the chair one more time, not frantic now but slow, deliberate, settling into it the way he had that first time in the garage — letting his weight sink into the cushion, letting his arms rest on the worn wood, letting his spine curve against the backrest.


"Please," he said. Quietly. Not a scream, not a demand. Just a word, small and honest, dropped into the silence.


The chair jumped.

Part Eight: Green
He landed in a meadow.


The shock of it — after the grey ocean, the grey sky, the grey hopelessness — was so overwhelming that Elliot couldn't process it at first. He sat in the chair and stared. Green. Everything was green. Grass, knee-high and rippling in a warm breeze. Wildflowers — yellow, white, purple — scattered through the grass like confetti. Trees in the distance, proper trees with broad leaves and rough bark, casting dappled shadows on the ground. And the sky — blue, achingly blue, with white clouds piled up on the horizon like mountains.


He could hear birds.


Birds.


He stood up from the chair and his legs gave way and he sat down hard in the grass and he laughed. He laughed and laughed, lying on his back in the meadow with the sun on his face and the smell of grass and earth and flowers filling his lungs, and the sound of birdsong — real, actual birdsong — ringing in his ears like a hymn.


He didn't know when this was. He didn't care. There were birds and grass and flowers and a warm sun and a blue sky, and it was so beautiful that it hurt, a physical ache in his chest like a hand squeezing his heart.


He drank from a stream that wound through the meadow, clear and cold and sweet. He picked berries from a bush — small, dark, tart — and ate them by the handful, the juice staining his fingers purple. He washed his face in the stream and saw his reflection for the first time in days. He looked terrible. Thin. Burnt. His eyes were too large in a face that had lost its softness, and there was a scratch on his cheek that he didn't remember getting, scabbed and angry.


He looked old. Not in years — he was still twelve, still a boy — but in the way that people look old when they've seen things they can't unsee. There was something in his eyes that hadn't been there before. A weight.


He stayed in the meadow for five days. It was the longest he'd stayed anywhere, and the most peaceful. There were no predators that he could see — no large animals at all, just birds and insects and, once, a small creature like a rabbit that watched him from the edge of the stream before vanishing into the grass.


He built a shelter of sorts — a lean-to of branches against a fallen tree, padded with grass. He made a fire, using a trick he'd seen in a survival show — a spindle of dry wood spun between his palms against a flat piece of bark, patient and persistent until a thin curl of smoke rose from the friction point and caught in a nest of dried grass. The fire was small, more smoke than flame, but it was his, and the warmth of it, the light of it in the gathering dusk, was the most comforting thing he'd felt since leaving home.


He ate berries and roots and, on the third day, a fish he caught in the stream by building a crude dam of stones and trapping it in the shallows. He cooked it over the fire on a flat stone, and it was the best thing he'd ever tasted. Better than Christmas dinner. Better than his birthday cake. Better than anything.


On the fifth night, he sat by the fire and looked up at the stars. They were different from the stars he knew — arranged in unfamiliar patterns, the constellations rearranged by the slow drift of the galaxy over millions of years. But they were still stars. The same stars, just seen from a different angle in time. The same light, travelling the same distances, arriving at the same small planet.


He thought about his parents. His mum would be frantic. His dad would be out looking, probably, walking the streets with a torch, calling his name. They'd have called the police by now. There'd be posters, maybe. Searches. The whole horrible machinery of a missing child, grinding into motion.


They wouldn't find him. Not in a million years. Not in four and a half billion.


The thought was a black pit, and he felt it open beneath him, and he let it. He sat by his fire under alien stars and let the grief take him, and it was vast and dark and bottomless, and he didn't fight it.


Then it passed. Like weather. Like everything.


He wiped his face. He banked the fire. He crawled into his shelter and slept, and in the morning, he ate the last of his berries, and he kicked dirt over the fire, and he sat in the chair.


The meadow vanished.

Part Nine: The Permian Extinction
He knew something was wrong the instant he arrived.


The air was poison. Not gradually, not subtly — immediately, violently wrong. It seared his throat and lungs like acid, and his eyes streamed tears, and he doubled over in the chair, coughing so hard he thought his ribs would crack. The sky was a toxic yellow-brown, and the ground was bare and scorched, and everywhere — everywhere — there was ash. It lay in drifts like grey snow, covering everything, filling the air with fine particles that hung in the sick light and made the world look like a faded photograph.


The Great Dying. The end-Permian extinction. The worst mass extinction in Earth's history, when ninety percent of all life on the planet was snuffed out in a geological instant. Volcanic eruptions on a scale beyond imagination, pouring carbon dioxide and sulphur into the atmosphere, turning the oceans acid and the air to poison.


Elliot couldn't breathe. The air was so thin, so depleted of oxygen and so thick with toxic gas, that each breath was a battle, a desperate gulp that brought more pain than relief. His vision blurred. His hands shook on the armrests.


He tried to stand. He couldn't. His legs were rubber. His chest was a knot of fire.


Sit, he thought. You're already sitting. Just — jump. Come on. Come on.


But the chair needed him to stand first. That was the rule. He had to break contact and re-establish it. He had to stand up and sit back down.


He planted his hands on the armrests and pushed. His arms trembled. The ash swirled around him, and through his streaming eyes he could see the landscape — a hellscape of cracked earth and dead trees, their trunks standing like blackened fingers against the poisoned sky. Nothing moved. Nothing lived. This was a world in the process of dying, and it was taking him with it.


He pushed harder. He got one foot under him, then the other. He straightened, swaying, his vision tunnelling, the air scraping his lungs with every breath.


He fell back into the chair.


The world changed.

Part Ten: The Jurassic
He lay gasping in the chair, dragging clean air into his abused lungs in great, shuddering gulps. Clean air. Warm, humid, oxygen-rich air that tasted of rain and green things and life. He coughed and coughed, and something dark came up — soot, maybe, or ash — and he spat it into the ferns and lay back and breathed.


When the world stopped spinning, he opened his eyes.


He was in a clearing surrounded by conifers — tall, straight-trunked trees with sprays of dark green needles. The ground was covered in a thick layer of fallen needles, soft and springy, and the air was warm and still. Somewhere nearby, water was running.


And there, at the edge of the clearing, looking at him with an expression of mild curiosity, was a dinosaur.


It was not a tyrannosaur. It was not a velociraptor. It was, in fact, rather boring-looking — about the size of a large dog, walking on two legs, with a long tail and a small head and a body covered in what looked like coarse, brownish feathers. It tilted its head and regarded him the way a pigeon might regard a dropped chip — with interest, but without urgency.


Elliot stared at it. It stared at him.


"Hi," he said. His voice was a croak.


The dinosaur made a sound — a soft, chirping trill — and went back to nosing through the leaf litter.


He laughed. It hurt his throat, but he laughed.


Over the next several days, Elliot discovered that this particular slice of the Jurassic was, by his standards, almost hospitable. The climate was warm and wet, with afternoon rains that filled the hollows in the rocks with fresh water. There were insects — normal-sized, mercifully — and the streams held small fish and crustaceans that he could catch. The feathered dinosaur, which he named Pip, appeared at the edge of his camp each morning and lingered until midday, apparently attracted by the scraps of fish that Elliot left near the tree line.


Pip was not a pet. Elliot knew that. Pip was a wild animal with a brain the size of a walnut, operating on instinct and opportunity, no more capable of affection than a lizard. But Pip came back, day after day, and sat in the ferns and made small chirping sounds, and when Elliot talked to it — which he did, increasingly, because the silence was a weight that pressed on him harder than the heat or the hunger — it tilted its head and listened, or seemed to listen, and that was enough.


"I'm from the future," Elliot told Pip on the fourth day, sitting by the stream with his feet in the water. "About a hundred and fifty million years in the future, give or take. There's a lot more of us there. People, I mean. Billions. We've got houses and cars and phones and — you have no idea what any of that means, do you?"


Pip trilled.


"We've got dogs," Elliot said. "They're a bit like you, actually. Friendlier, maybe. My dog's name is Biscuit. He's an idiot."


His voice cracked on the last word, and he stopped, and looked at the water, and breathed.


"I miss him," he said. "I miss everything."


Pip wandered off into the ferns. Elliot watched him go.


On the seventh day, the tyrannosaur came.


Not a tyrannosaur — wrong period, wrong continent, probably — but something large and bipedal and carnivorous, with a head like a hatchet and teeth like steak knives and an eye the colour of old amber that fixed on Elliot with an intensity that turned his blood to ice. It emerged from the trees at the far end of the clearing, thirty metres away, and it stopped, and it looked at him, and Elliot could see the intelligence in that gaze — not human intelligence, not reason, but something older and simpler and infinitely more dangerous: the cold, patient arithmetic of a predator calculating whether the small, strange creature in the clearing was worth the effort.


Elliot didn't breathe. He was standing by the stream, ten metres from the chair, and the predator was between them.


It took a step forward. The ground shook.


Elliot ran.


Not toward the chair — there was no path to the chair that didn't go through those teeth — but sideways, toward the thickest part of the conifer forest, where the trunks grew close together and the undergrowth was dense. He crashed through the ferns, branches whipping his face, needles in his hair, his feet slipping on the soft ground. Behind him, the predator moved. He heard it — the heavy, rhythmic thud of its footsteps, the crack of branches breaking under its weight, a low, huffing sound that might have been breathing or might have been something worse.


The trees saved him. They grew too close together for the predator's bulk, and it stopped at the edge of the dense forest, its massive head weaving between the trunks, that amber eye searching. Elliot pressed himself against a tree and held his breath and watched it through a gap in the branches. It stood there for a long time. Then it turned, with a surprising grace for something so large, and walked back into the clearing.


Toward the chair.


No.


The predator sniffed the chair. It nudged it with its snout. The chair rocked, tipped, and fell on its side in the ferns.


Elliot's heart stopped.


The predator lost interest. It moved on, through the clearing and into the trees on the far side, its tail swaying behind it like a slow pendulum. The sounds of its passage faded. The clearing was empty.


Elliot waited. He waited until his legs cramped and his hands went numb from gripping the tree trunk. He waited until the birds — small, feathered things that lived in the canopy — started singing again, which he had learned was a reliable sign that the large predators had moved on.


Then he crept out of the forest, crossed the clearing on legs that felt like stilts, and picked up the chair.


It was undamaged. A dinosaur had knocked it over and walked on it and sniffed it and it was fine. The fabric was intact, the legs solid, the copper wire still wrapped around the broken one. Even the strange symbol on the back was unchanged.


"You're tougher than you look," Elliot said.


He set the chair upright. He sat down.


He didn't say goodbye to Pip. Pip was nowhere to be seen — probably scattered by the predator, fled into the undergrowth to hide and wait. Elliot hoped Pip was okay. He knew it was foolish to worry about a small dinosaur in the Jurassic — they were built for this, adapted over millions of years to survive in a world of giants — but he worried anyway, because Pip had been his friend, or the closest thing to a friend that a hundred and fifty million years could offer.


The clearing vanished.

Part Eleven: The Drift
After the Jurassic, Elliot entered what he would later think of as the Drift.


Jump after jump, world after world, sliding through time like a stone skipping across water. He stopped counting. He stopped trying to identify the periods. He became a creature of pure reaction — land, assess, survive, move on. Each world was a problem to be solved: find water, find food, avoid death. The solutions varied. The urgency didn't.


He landed in a world of endless red desert where the only water was trapped in the stems of succulent plants that he split open with a sharp stone. He spent two days there before a sandstorm drove him back to the chair, his skin raw, his eyes clogged with grit.


He landed on a glacier — blue-white ice stretching in every direction, the cold so absolute that his wet clothes froze stiff within minutes. He jumped again immediately, and the shock of the transition — from ice to the sweltering heat of a tropical coast — sent his body into violent shivers that lasted for hours.


He landed in a shallow, warm sea and had to hold the chair above the water, standing in mud up to his knees, while small, armoured fish bumped against his legs. He fashioned a raft from driftwood and lashed the chair to it with strips of bark, and he floated for a day and a night under a sky packed with unfamiliar stars before the current brought him to a rocky shore.


He grew lean. His school uniform — already ruined — fell apart piece by piece. He replaced it with what he could find: animal skins from small creatures he managed to trap, leaves woven into crude fabric, strips of bark. He looked, he imagined, like a castaway from a children's book — wild-haired, sun-darkened, scarred. His shoes disintegrated on the third week — or what he estimated was the third week — and he went barefoot, his soles toughening until they were like leather.


His body changed. The softness of childhood burned away, replaced by a wiry, efficient thinness. Muscles he didn't know he had emerged in his arms, his legs, his back. His hands, once smooth and unblistered, became calloused and rough, the nails broken and dirty. He could build a fire in minutes now. He could read the weather by the colour of the sky, the behaviour of insects, the direction of the wind. He could identify water sources by the patterns of vegetation, the flight paths of birds, the subtle darkening of soil.


He was becoming something. Not the boy who had dragged a chair out of a thicket on Moss Lane. Something else. Something harder and quieter and more watchful.


He didn't like it.

Part Twelve: The Eocene
Then he landed in paradise.


Or what felt like paradise after months — he thought it might be months now — of survival. The Eocene, perhaps, or something close to it. A world of lush, subtropical forest, warm and green and teeming with life. Mammals were everywhere — small, darting things in the trees, larger shapes moving through the undergrowth. The air was full of birdsong, and the rivers ran clear and cold and full of fish.


And there were fruits. Actual fruits, hanging from the trees in clusters — small, round, yellow-orange things that tasted like a cross between an apricot and a mango. Elliot ate them until his stomach ached, juice running down his chin, laughing with a giddy, half-mad relief.


He built a proper camp. Not a lean-to this time, but something approaching a shelter — a frame of sturdy branches lashed together with vines, roofed with broad leaves, large enough to lie flat in. He dug a fire pit and lined it with stones. He fashioned a spear from a long, straight branch, sharpened to a point with a flat stone, and used it to catch fish from the river.


For the first time, he wasn't just surviving. He was living.


The small mammals were curious and unafraid — they had no reason to fear him; nothing in their evolutionary history had prepared them for a human. They came to his camp in the evenings, drawn by the scraps of food, and sat at the edges of the firelight, their eyes glinting. One of them — a creature about the size of a squirrel, with large eyes and delicate, grasping hands — became a regular visitor. Elliot called her Luna, because she always appeared at dusk, and because her eyes were round and silver in the firelight.


Luna was, as far as he could tell, some kind of early primate. She had a long, bushy tail and a face that was more lemur than monkey, and she moved through the branches with an acrobatic grace that made him smile. She wouldn't let him touch her, but she would sit an arm's length away and watch him eat, and sometimes she would make a soft, clicking sound that he chose to interpret as approval.


"You might be my great-great-great-times-a-million-grandmother," he told her one evening. "Wouldn't that be something?"


Luna clicked and vanished into the canopy.


He stayed in the Eocene for what he estimated was three weeks. Three weeks of warmth and food and the closest thing to companionship he'd had since Pip. Three weeks of sleeping through the night without waking in terror, of waking to birdsong instead of the sound of something large moving in the darkness.


He thought about staying forever.


The thought came to him one evening as he sat by the fire, full and warm, watching the stars appear between the leaves. He could stay. He could build a better shelter, learn the rhythms of this forest, grow old here in the warm, green heart of a world that didn't know his name. He could stop running. Stop hoping. Stop breaking his heart against the impossible mathematics of random time travel.


He could let go.


The thought was seductive. It whispered to him in the quiet moments, in the spaces between the birdsong and the crackle of the fire. Stay. Rest. This is good enough. This is more than you had any right to expect.


But it wasn't home. And Elliot, despite everything — despite the hunger and the fear and the loneliness that sat on his chest like a stone — hadn't given up on home. Not yet. Not quite.


On the twenty-second day, he packed what food he could carry — fruits wrapped in leaves, fish dried over the fire — and he sat in the chair, and he said goodbye to Luna, who watched him from a branch with her silver eyes.


"Wish me luck," he said.


She clicked.


He jumped.

Part Thirteen: Almost
He almost died in the Triassic.


The world he landed in was hot and arid, a landscape of cracked red earth and sparse, scrubby vegetation under a sky the colour of brass. The air was dry and carried a fine, gritty dust that got into everything — his eyes, his nose, his throat, the cracks in the chair's fabric. There was water, but not much — a thin, muddy creek that wound through a shallow valley, barely deep enough to wet his ankles.


He'd been there for two days when the pack found him.


They came at dawn, emerging from the scrub like shadows gaining substance. Six of them, the largest barely up to his waist, moving in a loose formation that spoke of coordination, of intelligence, of a social structure dedicated to one purpose: killing things.


They were coelophysoids, or something like them — bipedal predators, lightly built, with long necks and jaws full of small, serrated teeth. Individually, they were nothing. Together, they were a machine.


Elliot was by the creek, filling the hollowed-out gourd he'd made for carrying water. He saw them and froze.


They saw him and didn't.


The largest one — the leader, he assumed — made a sound. A high, staccato bark, almost like a dog. The others spread out, fanning into a semicircle, moving with a fluid, practised ease. They'd done this before. A thousand times, ten thousand. They were built for this.


Elliot dropped the gourd and ran.


The chair was fifty metres away, sitting where he'd left it in the shade of a gnarled, twisted tree. Fifty metres. He'd never been fast — always the last one picked for teams at school, always the one trailing at the back during cross-country — but fear was a fuel more potent than any he'd known, and he ran as he'd never run, his bare feet pounding the cracked earth, his breath ragged, the pack's barking chorus rising behind him.


He didn't look back. He knew they were there. He could hear them — the rapid patter of clawed feet, the staccato barks growing higher, more excited. They were gaining. Of course they were gaining. They were designed for this. They had sixty million years of evolution dedicated to running things down.


Thirty metres. Twenty. Ten.


He threw himself into the chair.


The world went white.

Part Fourteen: The Long Quiet
He landed somewhere dark. Underground, maybe — a cave, low-ceilinged and close, the air cool and damp and smelling of mineral and old water. He couldn't see. He sat in the chair in perfect blackness and listened to his own breathing and the distant drip of water and nothing else.


He was bleeding. One of the coelophysoids had caught his leg — a shallow gash across the back of his calf that stung viciously and was wet and warm. He pressed his hand against it and felt the blood, slick between his fingers.


He needed light. He needed to clean the wound. He needed —


He needed to stop. Just for a moment. Just stop.


Elliot leaned back in the chair. The darkness pressed against his open eyes. The water dripped. His heart slowed from its frantic hammering to something steadier, and the adrenaline drained from his body, leaving behind a fatigue so deep it felt like the gravity had been turned up.


He was so tired.


Not just physically, though his body ached in places he didn't know could ache, and the cut on his leg throbbed with each heartbeat. He was tired in a deeper way — a soul-tiredness, a tiredness of the spirit that no amount of sleep could fix. He was tired of being afraid. Tired of being hungry. Tired of waking each morning to a world that didn't know him, that had no place for him, that would kill him as indifferently as a boot kills an ant.


He was tired of being brave.


"I can't do this," he said to the dark. "I can't do this anymore."


The dark didn't answer. The water dripped.


He sat there for a long time. Hours, maybe. He slept, or something close to sleep — a grey, formless drift that wasn't quite unconsciousness but wasn't quite awareness either. When he surfaced, the cave was still dark, and the water was still dripping, and the cut on his leg had stopped bleeding but had stiffened into a crust that cracked when he moved.


He thought about not getting up. About staying in the chair, in the dark, in the silence, and letting whatever happened happen. The chair couldn't move if he didn't stand up, and he didn't have to stand up, and maybe that was a kind of peace — to stop, finally, to just stop.


But then he thought about his mother. Not a memory, exactly — more like a sensation. The smell of her perfume. The sound of her voice calling him down for dinner. The way she pushed his hair back from his forehead when she thought he was asleep.


He stood up.


The pain in his leg was bright and immediate, and he gritted his teeth against it and felt along the cave wall with his hands. The rock was rough and damp. He followed it, limping, one hand on the wall and one outstretched, until the darkness began to thin and a grey light appeared ahead — a crack, a fissure, the cave's mouth.


He went back for the chair. He dragged it, scraping and bumping, through the narrow cave and out into the light.


He was on a hillside. Below him, a river wound through a valley of green, and the sky was blue, and somewhere in the trees a bird was singing.


He cleaned his leg in the river. He tore a strip from what remained of his shirt and bound the wound. He ate berries from a bush by the water's edge.


Then he sat in the chair, and he jumped.

Part Fifteen: Voices
He didn't know how long he'd been travelling. The days blurred together, punctuated by moments of terror and stretches of grinding, monotonous survival. He'd stopped trying to count. He'd stopped trying to guess the periods. He'd stopped doing a lot of things.


But he hadn't stopped hoping. Not entirely. The hope lived in him like a pilot light — small, flickering, barely there, but never quite going out.


And then, on a jump that felt like any other, he heard voices.


Not animal sounds. Not wind. Not water. Voices. Human voices, distant but unmistakable — the rising and falling cadence of speech, of language, of people talking to each other.


Elliot was standing in a field of tall grass on the edge of a broad, flat plain. The sun was high and hot, and the grass rippled in the wind like a golden sea. And from beyond a low ridge to the south, the voices came — faint, carried on the breeze, impossible and real.


He ran. He forgot the chair — forgot everything — and ran toward the sound, crashing through the grass, his heart slamming against his ribs. The ridge was low, barely a fold in the land, and he scrambled up it on hands and knees and looked down.


There, in a shallow valley by a bend in a river, was a camp.


Shelters made of branches and animal skins. A fire, thin smoke rising. Figures moving — men, women, children. People. People. Real, breathing, talking, laughing people, dressed in skins, carrying tools, existing in the same moment and the same place as him.


Elliot sank to his knees in the grass. His vision blurred with tears. He couldn't speak. He couldn't breathe. He could only kneel and watch and feel the enormous, crushing, unbearable relief of not being alone.


He didn't approach. Not immediately. He watched from the ridge as the day unfolded — the women gathering plants by the river, the men working on something near the fire, the children running and shouting and playing a game that involved throwing sticks at a target. They were human. Early human — their features heavier than his, their bodies stocky and powerful, their tools crude but effective. Neanderthals, maybe. Or early Homo sapiens. It didn't matter. They were people.


He approached at dusk, walking slowly down the ridge with his hands open and visible. They saw him. The camp went quiet. Faces turned toward him — wary, curious, uncertain. A man stepped forward, tall and broad-shouldered, carrying a spear.


Elliot stopped. He raised his hands higher.


"I'm not going to hurt you," he said, knowing they couldn't understand the words but hoping they could understand the tone. "I'm just — I'm alone. I've been alone for a long time."


The man studied him. His eyes moved over Elliot's ragged clothes, his thin frame, his bare feet, the makeshift bandage on his leg. Something in his face shifted — the wariness softened, not into trust, but into something adjacent to it. Recognition, perhaps. The recognition of one struggling creature by another.


He lowered his spear. He said something — a short, guttural phrase — and gestured toward the fire.


Elliot walked into the camp.

Part Sixteen: The Tribe
They couldn't speak to each other. Not with words. But humans had been communicating long before language — with gestures, expressions, the shared grammar of the body — and Elliot learned quickly.


They called themselves something that sounded like a cough followed by a click. Elliot called them the River People, because they lived by the river and because he needed a name for them and that was the simplest one.


They were kind. Cautious, practical, unsentimental — but kind, in the way that people are kind when kindness is a survival strategy, when the group's strength depends on the welfare of each member. They fed him. Thick, gamey stew from a clay pot, heavy with roots and chunks of meat he didn't try to identify. They gave him a place by the fire, and a skin to sleep under, and an older woman — grey-haired, with strong hands and a face like weathered stone — examined his leg wound and packed it with a poultice of crushed herbs that stung like fire but drew the redness out within days.


He stayed with them. Days turned into weeks. He learned their routines — the morning hunt, the afternoon gathering, the evening fire around which the whole group assembled to eat and talk and, sometimes, sing. The songs were unlike anything he'd heard — wordless, rhythmic, a kind of communal humming that rose and fell with the firelight, beautiful and eerie, like hearing music for the very first time.


He made himself useful. He couldn't hunt — he lacked the strength, the skill, the knowledge of the land — but he could gather, and he could carry, and he could tend the fire. The children found him fascinating. They followed him around, touching his strange clothes, his pale skin, his dark hair. One of them — a girl of about five, with a round face and quick, dark eyes — appointed herself his shadow, appearing at his elbow whenever he turned around, grinning up at him with a gap-toothed smile.


He called her Sparrow, because she was small and quick and everywhere at once.


And the chair sat at the edge of the camp, by the river, collecting dust and leaves, and Elliot tried not to look at it.


Because he was happy. For the first time since Moss Lane, since the garage, since the Tuesday afternoon that had become the dividing line of his life — before and after, home and not-home — he was happy. He had food and warmth and shelter and the company of other human beings, and the simple, animal pleasure of belonging somewhere, of being part of something larger than himself.


But it wasn't home. And the chair was there, sitting by the river, waiting.


On the thirty-ninth day — he was counting again, because counting gave shape to the time, gave him something to hold onto — Sparrow brought him a gift. A necklace, crudely made, of small shells strung on a strip of sinew. She held it out to him with both hands, her face solemn, and said something he couldn't understand.


He took it. He put it on. The shells were cool against his skin.


"Thank you," he said.


Sparrow grinned.


That night, by the fire, the man who had met him on the ridge — the one Elliot thought of as the Chief, though he was probably just the most competent hunter — sat beside him and pointed at the chair.


He said something. His tone was questioning.


Elliot looked at the chair. In the firelight, the faded burgundy fabric looked almost warm, almost alive. The symbol on the back — the circle with the intersecting lines — caught the light and seemed to shift.


"It's how I got here," Elliot said. "It's how I'll get home. If I can."


The Chief didn't understand the words. But he understood the look on Elliot's face — the pull, the longing, the war between staying and going — because he reached out and put his hand on Elliot's shoulder, briefly, firmly, and said something that sounded, impossibly, like good luck.


Elliot left the next morning. He walked to the river's edge, where the chair sat in the early light, and he turned back to look at the camp. Sparrow was standing outside her shelter, watching him. The shell necklace hung against his chest.


He raised his hand.


She raised hers.


He sat in the chair.

Part Seventeen: Descent
The jumps after the River People were the worst.


He landed in places that seemed designed to punish hope. A world choked by volcanic winter, the sky a permanent twilight, the ground buried under metres of ash. A frozen ocean under a sun so dim it looked like a dying coal. A landscape of bare rock and acid rain that ate through his makeshift clothes and left red welts on his skin.


He landed in the Cretaceous again — or something like it — and this time there was no gentle clearing, no curious Pip. He appeared in the middle of a floodplain, flat and exposed, and before he could orient himself there was a sound like thunder and the ground shook and a herd of something enormous came stampeding through, their bodies like moving walls of muscle and bone, and he threw himself flat under the chair and the herd parted around him by inches, their feet shaking the earth, their bellies passing over him so close he could smell them — a warm, grassy, animal smell — and then they were gone, and he was alive, and he lay in the churned mud and stared at the sky and tried to remember what hope felt like.


He ran out of food. The dried fish and fruits from his various stops ran out, and the worlds he landed in offered nothing — no plants, no animals, no water. He went three days without eating. His body consumed itself — he could feel it, the muscles dissolving, the fat (what little remained) burning away. His thoughts became slow, fuzzy, disconnected. He had conversations with people who weren't there — his mother, his father, Biscuit, Pip, Sparrow. They sat with him in the chair and talked to him, and he answered, and it was only later, when the hallucination passed, that he realised he'd been talking to empty air.


On the fourth day without food, he landed on a rocky shore where the tide pools were full of mussels. He smashed them open with a stone and ate them raw — briny, slimy, gritty with sand — and they were so good he cried.


He started talking to the chair.


Not in the way he'd talked to Pip or Luna — not as a game, not as a way to fill the silence. He talked to it because he'd begun to believe it was listening. The chair had rules. The chair had patterns. The chair responded to his state of mind — or seemed to, in the way that machines sometimes seem to respond to their operators, not through intelligence but through some deeper resonance. It jumped when he was calm. It refused when he was frantic. It had saved him, more than once, by delivering him from death to safety with a timing that felt less like chance and more like intention.


"Where are you taking me?" he asked one night, sitting in the chair on a hilltop under a sky ablaze with more stars than he'd ever seen. "Are you trying to get me home? Or is this random? Am I just — bouncing?"


The chair didn't answer. The stars burned.


"I think you're trying," Elliot said. "I think you've been trying all along. You just don't know the way either."


He touched the symbol on the back. The circle with the lines. A compass that had forgotten which way was north.


"We'll find it," he said. "Together."

Part Eighteen: The Ice
Then the ice age came, and it almost ended everything.


He arrived in a world of white. Not the brief, brutal ice field he'd landed on early in his journey — this was something vaster, something permanent, a world locked in the grip of a glaciation that had been going on for thousands of years and would go on for thousands more. Ice covered everything. The mountains, the valleys, the rivers, the plains — all of it buried under a sheet of ice so thick it had reshaped the land, carving new valleys, filling old ones, grinding the rock beneath it into powder.


The cold was absolute. It was the kind of cold that doesn't just chill you — it empties you, reaching inside your body and pulling the warmth out like thread from a spool. Elliot felt it the instant he arrived, a cold so profound it was almost a sound, a low, bass note that vibrated in his bones.


He had no fire. No shelter. No food. The clothes on his back — a patchwork of animal skins and woven bark — were designed for tropical heat, not glacial cold. Within minutes, his fingers were numb. Within an hour, he couldn't feel his feet.


He walked. There was nothing else to do. The chair was too heavy to carry far, so he dragged it, leaving a track in the snow behind him, shuffling forward with the single-minded determination of a body that has decided to live regardless of what the mind thinks about it.


He walked all day. The sun — visible for once, a pale disc in a sky the colour of iron — crawled along the horizon and set, and the temperature, already murderous, dropped further, and the stars came out, and the cold closed in like a fist.


He built a shelter. Barely deserving of the word — a hollow scooped in the snow, just large enough for him and the chair, roofed with blocks of ice cut with a sharp stone. Inside, out of the wind, the temperature was still well below freezing, but the chair's fabric — that ancient, indestructible burgundy — held some warmth, or seemed to, and he curled in it and pulled a slab of ice across the entrance and shivered in the dark.


He didn't sleep. Sleep, he knew, was death. The cold would take him if he slept — quietly, gently, the way it always did, slowing the heart and dimming the mind until both stopped. He had to stay awake. He had to keep moving, keep thinking, keep the small flame of consciousness burning.


He thought about home. He pictured his bedroom — the models on the shelf, the poster on the wall, the pile of salvaged junk in the corner. He pictured the kitchen — the yellow walls, the table with the wobbly leg, the fridge with its collection of magnets from places his family had visited. He pictured Biscuit, asleep on the sofa, his legs twitching in some dream of rabbits.


He held these pictures in his mind like coals, and they warmed him, and he made it through the night.


In the morning, stiff and frost-bitten and barely able to stand, he lowered himself into the chair.


The ice vanished.

Part Nineteen: The Door
He was in a house.


Not a cave, not a shelter, not a hollow in the snow — a house. Walls of rough stone, a floor of packed earth, a ceiling of wooden beams. A fire burning in a crude hearth, casting wavering light across a room filled with things — clay pots, animal skins, stone tools, bundles of dried herbs hanging from the ceiling.


Elliot sat in the chair in the middle of this room and stared.


There was a doorway — a low, rectangular opening covered by a hanging skin. Through it, he could hear sounds. Wind. Voices. The bleating of goats.


Goats.


He stood up. His legs buckled, and he caught himself on the chair and stood again, swaying. He crossed the room on feet he couldn't feel and pushed aside the hanging skin.


He was looking at a village. A cluster of stone houses, maybe twenty or thirty, arranged around a central area of beaten earth. There were people — men and women dressed in rough wool and leather, carrying tools and pots and bundles of firewood. There were animals — goats, definitely, penned in a rough enclosure, and something that might have been a dog trotting along the edge of the buildings. And beyond the village, spreading to the horizon — fields. Cultivated fields, rows of green plants growing in dark earth, stretching away toward a line of hills under a sky of soft, milky grey.


Agriculture. Domesticated animals. Permanent settlement.


This was recent. This was so recent. Not millions of years. Not hundreds of thousands. This was thousands — maybe only a few thousand — years from home.


Elliot's knees gave way. He sank down in the doorway and wrapped his arms around himself and shook, not with cold this time but with something that had no name — a feeling too big for his body, too much for his twelve-year-old frame. He was close. He was so close. The sliver of human history, the impossibly thin band of time that held everything he loved — he was inside it. He was inside it.


A woman appeared in the central area. She saw him. She stopped. She called out — a sharp, questioning sound — and others came, and they gathered around him, and their faces were open and curious and human, and Elliot looked up at them from the doorway and said the only thing he could think of:


"Please. I need to sit down."


They took him in. They fed him — flat bread, still warm from baking, and a thick, savoury porridge of grains and herbs. They wrapped him in blankets of rough-spun wool that scratched his skin and held the warmth. The woman who had found him — tall, with dark hair pulled back from a strong, angular face — sat beside him and spoke to him in a language he didn't understand, her tone gentle, her hands moving in gestures that needed no translation: eat, rest, you're safe.


He stayed for a week. He helped in the fields, pulling weeds and carrying water, and the villagers accepted his help with the matter-of-fact pragmatism of people for whom an extra pair of hands was always welcome. They didn't ask where he'd come from. Perhaps they assumed he was a traveller, a wanderer from another settlement. Perhaps they didn't care. He was there, and he was willing to work, and that was enough.


But he knew he had to keep going. He was close — centuries, maybe, from his own time — and the chair was waiting in the stone house, and the longer he stayed, the harder it would be to leave.


On the seventh day, he stood in the doorway of the stone house and looked at the chair. The fabric was more faded than ever, the stuffing poking through in new places, the wooden legs scarred and battered by a journey that had spanned the entirety of Earth's history. The symbol on the back — the circle and the lines — seemed to glow faintly in the dim light, though that might have been his imagination.


He sat down.


"Home," he whispered. "Take me home."


He closed his eyes.

Part Twenty: Moss Lane
The first thing he noticed was the smell.


Cut grass. Car exhaust. The faint, chemical sweetness of fabric softener from someone's open window. Smells so ordinary, so achingly familiar, that his brain refused to process them. He sat in the chair with his eyes closed and breathed, and the smells washed over him, and something inside him — some wall he'd built, some dam he'd erected to hold back the grief — cracked, and broke, and fell.


He opened his eyes.


He was in a garden. His garden. The back garden of 14 Moss Lane, with its patchy lawn and the washing line and the fence with the broken slat and the shed where his father kept the lawnmower. The sky was grey — English grey, the grey of a September afternoon — and the air was cool and damp, and a blackbird was singing in the apple tree, and from inside the house he could hear the television.


The television.


Elliot stood up from the chair. His legs — legs that had carried him across billions of years, that had run from predators and climbed mountains and walked across glaciers — trembled. He took a step. Another. The grass was soft under his bare, calloused feet. The back door was three metres away. Two. One.


He pushed it open.


The kitchen was exactly as he'd left it. The yellow walls. The table with the wobbly leg. The fridge with its magnets. A mug on the counter, half-full of cold tea. And on the floor by the radiator, sleeping, his legs twitching in some dream of rabbits —


"Biscuit."


The dog's head came up. His ears pricked. He looked at Elliot — at the wild hair, the tattered skins, the shell necklace, the scars — and for a moment, he didn't recognise him. Then his tail started to move. Slowly at first, tentatively, then faster, sweeping the floor in great, joyful arcs, and he scrambled to his feet and crossed the kitchen and threw himself at Elliot's legs, and Elliot fell to his knees and wrapped his arms around the dog's warm, solid, gloriously ordinary body and buried his face in his fur and wept.


"Elliot?"


His mother's voice. From the doorway. He looked up.


She was standing there in her dressing gown, a cup of tea in her hand, her face a mask of confusion that was already, even as he watched, rearranging itself into something else — into shock, into fear, into the beginning of a relief so enormous it would take months to fully arrive.


"Hi, Mum," he said.


The cup fell. Tea splashed across the tiles. She crossed the kitchen in two strides and pulled him to her, and she was shaking, her whole body shaking, and she was saying his name over and over — Elliot, Elliot, Elliot — and he held on to her the way he'd held on to the chair in the worst moments, with everything he had, with every part of himself.


"Where have you been?" she whispered.


Elliot looked over her shoulder, through the kitchen window, at the garden. The chair was still there, sitting on the patchy lawn, its faded burgundy fabric dark with dew, its broken leg splinted with copper wire. Just a chair. Just an old, ugly, beaten-up chair.


"It's a long story," he said.

Epilogue
He told them, eventually. Not everything — not the hunger, not the terror, not the nights he'd wanted to stop — but enough. Enough for them to understand why their son had disappeared for three days (it had been three days, his mother said; only three days, though it had felt like months) and come back looking like he'd aged ten years.


They didn't believe him. Of course they didn't. His father smiled the careful smile of a man humoring his traumatized child. His mother held his hand and nodded and made an appointment with a therapist. The police came and went, satisfied that the boy had returned and reluctant to investigate further.


The chair stayed in the garden. His parents wanted to throw it away, but Elliot wouldn't let them. He didn't sit in it again — not once, in all the years that followed — but he kept it, first in the garden, then in the garage, then, when he was older and had a house of his own, in a corner of his study, where it sat beneath a window and collected dust and looked like nothing.


He kept the shell necklace, too. Sparrow's gift, strung on ancient sinew, the shells worn smooth by time. He kept it in a drawer by his bed, and sometimes, on nights when the world felt too small and too quiet and too safe, he took it out and held it and remembered.


He never forgot the River People. Or Pip. Or Luna. Or the Devonian beach where a small, brave creature had looked at him with round eyes and then crawled back into the sea. He never forgot the meadow, the fire, the stars. He never forgot the ice, the darkness, the cave where he had almost stopped.


He never forgot the chair.


Years later — decades — he would sit in his study and look at it and think about the symbol on its back. The circle with the intersecting lines. Not a compass, he thought now. A clock. A clock with no hands, no numbers, no fixed point. A clock that measured not hours or minutes but eons — the slow, vast, incomprehensible sweep of deep time, of which a human life was less than a heartbeat.


He'd lived inside that heartbeat. He'd crossed the void on either side of it, the billions of years of fire and ice and emptiness, and he'd come back.


The chair sat in its corner, patient and silent, and the dust settled on its faded fabric, and the light moved across the floor, and the years passed, quick and bright and ordinary, and Elliot Wynn — survivor, traveller, the boy who had seen the whole of time — grew old.


He never sat in the chair again.


But sometimes, late at night, when the house was quiet and the world outside was dark, he would stand in the doorway of his study and look at it, and he would swear — he would swear — that the symbol on its back was glowing.


Just faintly. Just enough.


As if it were waiting.
